Height and weight in Brazil

In Brazil, if you are a man, military conscription is mandatory when you turn 18. Thus, every year, hundred of thousands of young men show up in a military unit in a given day at 7am. There you are measured and weighted (among other things, the whole process takes the whole morning). Although the process is deeply annoying, it generates millions of lines of data. With it, I will try to answer an old question:

Is there a relation between height and weight?

For this, I downloaded and analyzed 10 datasets with information from the conscription process, one for each year between 2008 and 2017. To make the analysis scientific valid, I only used information about men with 18 years old (in some cases, you can delay your participation in the process. These cases were ignored).

Let's start simple: a scatter plot . Used to check correlations, it is the perfect way to start analyzing the information we have, no? One axis will be the height, the other the weight. If the variables have a correlation, the dots should form more or less a line. It is not the case here. Each dot is a 18 year old boy that woke up really early in a given day of the selected year. For the visualization, a random sample of 5000 persons is selected because otherwise, there would be far to many dots (more than 300.000)

You probably do not know this, but the height and the weight are not the only information in the database. There is a third variable: the state where the person lives (Brazil has states, like the USA). A bar chart allow us to compare the average weight and height per state and year to see how it varies geographically.

If you are still paying attention after all this time, a valid point that you could make is that the previous chart compared averages and averages can be misleading. An example: the average temperature of the Sahara desert is 30 degrees celsius. To avoid this trap, let's check the maximun and minimum value for each Brazilian state. For this, we use a dot plot.
